Guidelines

How the examiner reaches a verdict, and how much to trust it.

Text examinations

Pasted text is sent to a language model with a prompt asking it to weigh linguistic signals of machine vs. human writing — sentence rhythm, word choice, structural tells — and return a strict verdict with a confidence score and a one-line rationale. This is a model opinion, not a lab test: there's no ground truth to check it against.

Image examinations

Image verdicts combine two independent evidence sources:

  • Deterministic forensics — the file's own metadata is scanned for AI-tool fingerprints (embedded generation parameters, named generators, Content Credentials markers) and camera EXIF completeness.
  • Vision-model judgment — the same model looks at the image itself for visual tells, with the forensic findings given as context.

A strong metadata fingerprint overrides a contradicting visual read. Absence of metadata proves nothing on its own — screenshots and social-media re-saves strip it from real photos just as readily as from AI images.

Reading the evidence tags

Strong — close to conclusive on its own (e.g. an embedded generation-parameters block).
Moderate — meaningfully shifts the verdict but isn't decisive alone.
Weak — a minor signal folded into the overall score.

Honest limitation

LLM-based AI detection is inherently probabilistic and not forensically reliable. Treat every verdict here as a second opinion, not proof — and weigh image verdicts by whether the "why" behind them cites hard metadata or a visual impression.
